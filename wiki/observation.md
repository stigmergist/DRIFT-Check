# Observation

Observation is the primary source of truth in DRIFT.

The emphasis here is practical. What you can see in behaviour, outcomes, and recurring patterns is a stronger foundation for **[judgement](judgement.md)** than intent, labels, or plans. People can be sincere and still wrong about what is happening. Plans can be detailed and still disconnected from operational reality. Observation anchors interpretation in what the system is actually doing.

Good observation is specific. It points to repeatable **[signals](signals_and_noise.md)** rather than isolated events. It looks at what people say, what they do, and what results appear. When these diverge, the divergence itself is information.

Observation quality is key to using existing frameworks well. See [method_mapping.md](method_mapping.md) for how to apply DRIFT thinking alongside your existing methods. Observation is the bridge that helps you test whether a framework choice is working in your actual conditions.

Observation is also time-sensitive. In fast-changing systems, old observations lose validity quickly. Late accuracy can become functional error if conditions have already shifted.

A useful rule is simple: if you cannot point to **[observable](observation.md)** behaviour, treat your assessment as weak. That does not mean you must wait for perfect evidence. It means you should acknowledge **[uncertainty](uncertainty.md)** and avoid acting as if assumptions were facts.
