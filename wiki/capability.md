# Capability

Capability is the system's ability to produce an outcome reliably and efficiently.

That definition is precise in a way that matters.
It is not about having the right people, tools, or process in isolation.
It is about whether those elements combine to produce the needed outcome, consistently and predictably.
If the outcome happens only sometimes, you do not yet have a capability.
You have ingredients, not a functioning system.

The two parts of the definition, *reliably* and *efficiently*, are not the same.
Confusing them causes trouble.
Reliability means the outcome is produced when needed, with acceptable consistency.
Efficiency means it is produced without unnecessary cost, time, or effort.
You need reliability first.
Efficiency only matters once output is consistently reliable.

Capability also requires **[agency](agency.md)**.
If outcomes depend on luck, external factors, or one hero, this is not a real capability.
It is a **[fragile](fragility.md)** arrangement that happens to work right now.
Real capability is reproducible and survives disruption.

## What makes a capability work

Capability is enabled by the interaction of five interdependent elements:

**People** — skills, judgement, and behaviour. When teams lack skill or turnover is high, outcomes become inconsistent and reactive firefighting increases.

**Process** — how work actually flows. When process is unclear or varies person-to-person, results become dependent on who is available and outcomes drift.

**Information** — what is known, shared, and used.
When critical information lives in silos or disappears when people leave, decisions become brittle and repetitive problems recur.

**Technology and tools** — what enables or constrains execution.
When tools are unreliable, slow, or workaround-heavy, effort rises and outcomes become slower or more error-prone.

**Governance** — how decisions are made and controlled. When decision authority is unclear or inconsistent, coordination overhead rises and strategic intent gets reinterpreted in execution.

No single element defines capability on its own.
Capability emerges from their interaction in producing consistent outcomes.
If one element is weak, the whole system depends on extra effort or luck.
This is why capability degrades gradually: people leave, workarounds replace designed process, tools drift, information gets isolated, and governance becomes informal.
Each change seems small, but together they reduce reliability.

## How capability degrades

Capabilities do not hold their state passively. They need active maintenance. People leave. Workarounds become default practice. External dependencies shift. Early degradation signals are subtle: more variability, more incidents, more heroics. These signals are easy to miss when headline metrics still look acceptable.

## Why this matters for action

The **[state](state.md)** of a capability determines what kind of action will help it. This is one of DRIFT's central ideas. You cannot **[optimise](optimise.md)** something that is not yet reliable. You should not simplify something you do not yet control. Organisations avoid **[misfit](misfit.md)** by matching action to capability state.
