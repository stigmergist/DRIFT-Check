## 1) Core Framing (how to think about situations)
- **Context decides whether it makes sense to act**, not what to do  
- **Capability is the system’s ability to produce an outcome — the aim is to do it reliably and efficiently**  
- The **observable state of a capability determines what kind of action will actually work**  
- **Most failure comes from actions that do not match the situation**  
- The system improves when the **right action matches the actual observed state**, not when effort increases  
- **Action should be judged by intent** (stabilising, simplifying, improving), not by description  
- **Observations reveal the state of the context, the state of the capability, and what is being done to it**  
- Actions can improve the system in the **short term while degrading it in the long term**  
- The **time horizon of impact** determines whether an action is truly improving the system  
- Short-term performance and long-term stability are often in tension  
- Not all constraints can be changed; some must be **worked within rather than improved**  
- Effort spent against fixed constraints produces **friction, not progress**  

---

## 2) Core Anchor
- **Most failure comes from actions that do not match the situation**  
- The **observable state of the capability tells you what action will work**  
- **Misfit is the gap between the two**  
- There is a tension between **moving quickly** and **ensuring correct fit**  
- Acting too early creates misfit; acting too late loses opportunity  
- The system must balance **speed of action** with **accuracy of fit**  

---

## 3) Definitions
- **Context** is whether it is **appropriate, safe, and worthwhile to act**  
- **Capability** is the system’s ability to **produce an outcome reliably and efficiently**  
- **State** is what the system is **actually doing, not what is intended**  
- **Observation** is what can be seen in **behaviour, outcomes, and patterns**  
- **Action** is what is being **done to the system, best understood by its intent**  
- **Misfit** is the gap between **observed state and applied action**  
- **Value** is whether an outcome is **actually used to create impact**  
- **Progress** is improvement in the system’s **ability to produce outcomes reliably and efficiently**  
- **Stability** is when outcomes are **predictable without constant intervention**  
- **Complexity** is when the same outcome is achieved in **multiple different ways**  
- **Uncertainty** is when the same action produces **different outcomes**  
- **Alignment** is shared understanding that produces **consistent behaviour**  
- **Scaling** is increasing volume or reach, which **amplifies existing behaviour**  
- **Absorption capacity** is the ability to **survive being wrong repeatedly**  
- **Fragility** is when small issues **propagate into larger failures**  
- **Programme** is a **hypothesis about how change will create value**  
- **Reversibility** is how easily an action can be undone if it is wrong  
- **Irreversibility** is when an action creates lasting constraints or consequences  
- The **cost of being wrong** depends more on reversibility than uncertainty  
- **Agency** is the ability to **change the system or influence its behaviour**  
- A problem without agency is a **constraint to navigate, not a system to optimise**  

---

## 4) Observation (source of truth)
- **Observations are the primary source of truth**, not plans, labels, or intent  
- Observations reveal the **state of the context (should we act?)**  
- Observations reveal the **state of the capability (what action fits?)**  
- Observations reveal **what actions are already being applied**  
- **Misfit is visible when observed actions do not match observed state**  
- If you cannot point to **observable behaviour**, you are likely working from **assumption, not reality**  
- Observations degrade in validity when the system is **changing rapidly**  
- In fast-changing environments, **timeliness of observation matters as much as accuracy**  

---

## 5) Organisation as a System (structure and value flow)
- Treat the organisation as a **stack of capabilities delivering value upward to the user**  
- Value is realised at the top but **created and constrained by what sits below**  
- What happens at the top is a **reflection of the condition underneath**  
- A **capability stack** is a layered system where **each level depends on the one below**  
- A capability only exists if it can **actually produce outcomes in practice**  
- Capabilities combine **people, process, technology, and governance into something that works**  
- **Local optimisation is not success** if it makes the wider system worse  
- Weak foundations propagate upward as **distorted or unstable outcomes**  
- Improvements must be evaluated at the **system level, not just the local level**  
- A local improvement that degrades the wider system is **not an improvement**  
- The system is only improved when **overall value flow improves**  
- **system improvement may require co-ordinated improvement at multiple points in the capability stack at the same time**

---

## 6) Capability Model (what kind of action fits)
- The **state of the capability determines what kind of action will work**  
- **Stabilise** → restore the ability to **produce outcomes reliably**  
- **Rationalise** → reduce **duplication and inconsistency in how outcomes are produced**  
- **Optimise** → improve **efficiency and performance of already reliable outcomes**  
- You cannot optimise what you cannot yet **reliably produce**  
- You should not simplify what you do not yet **control**  
- Optimisation only works when outcomes are **consistent and dependable**  
- **Variability means the capability is not yet reliable**  
- If the same action gives different results, the capability is **not yet understood or controlled**  
- If different actions give the same result, the capability contains **unnecessary complexity**  
- Systems operate in different modes:  
  - **Learning mode** → understanding cause and effect  
  - **Performance mode** → improving efficiency of known outcomes  
- Confusing these modes leads to **premature optimisation or endless analysis**  

---

## 7) Context Model (when and whether to act)
- **Proceed** → the situation is **understood and aligned enough to act**  
- **Align** → people are **not working from the same understanding**  
- **Probe** → cause and effect are **unclear and need testing**  
- **Stop** → it is **not clear this is worth doing at all**  
- If people interpret the same decision differently, you have **misalignment**  
- If outcomes vary unpredictably, you are in **uncertainty**  
- If value is unclear, improvement effort increases **waste, not impact**  

---

## 8) Misfit and Failure Patterns
- **Misfit is when the type of action does not match the state of the capability**  
- Misfit is visible when **what is being done differs from what the capability needs**  
- **Automating instability makes unreliable capabilities fail faster and at scale**  
- **Standardising before alignment creates multiple inconsistent versions of the same capability**  
- **Simplifying unstable capabilities removes buffers and increases fragility**  
- **Optimising unreliable capabilities amplifies inconsistency, not performance**  
- Most systems fail gradually through **persistent misaligned action**  
- Misfit compounds over time as actions reinforce the wrong conditions  
- Small misalignments accumulate into **systemic failure**  
- Early correction is cheaper than **late-stage recovery**  

---

## 9) Scaling, Risk, and Absorption
- **Scaling amplifies how the capability behaves, not just how much it produces**  
- Do not scale a capability that cannot yet **reliably produce outcomes**  
- Scaling confusion creates **faster divergence and larger problems**  
- You can act with misfit only if you have the **capacity to absorb the consequences**  
- **Absorption capacity is the ability to survive being wrong repeatedly**  
- High absorption capacity enables learning; low capacity leads to **rapid breakdown**  

---

## 10) Programmes, Measurement, and Progress
- A **programme is a hypothesis about how capabilities will create value**  
- Programmes should aim to **demonstrate real outcomes early**  
- If a capability cannot yet produce outcomes, the programme has **not created value**  
- **KPIs and reporting show activity and speed, not capability or value**  
- Progress is improvement in the system’s **ability to produce outcomes reliably and efficiently**  
- Effort should follow **value and readiness, not habit or sunk cost**  
- Effort is only useful when it improves the system’s ability to produce outcomes  
- Increasing effort without improving capability increases **cost, not progress**  
- The system should direct effort toward areas of **highest value and readiness**  

---

## 11) Signals and Heuristics
- **Stability** → outcomes are **predictable and controlled**  
- **Complexity** → same outcome, **many different approaches**  
- **Uncertainty** → same action, **different outcomes**  
- **Consistency** → ready for **improvement**  
- **Variability** → needs **stabilisation or understanding**  
- “This should be working” → **instability**  
- “Everyone does it differently” → **complexity**  
- “We’re not sure why” → **uncertainty**  
- “Why are we doing this?” → **low value**  
- Not all observations are equally reliable; systems produce both **signal and noise**  
- Decisions should favour **repeatable patterns over isolated events**  
- Misreading noise as signal leads to **incorrect action**  

---

## Core Idea
- **Capability = ability to produce an outcome**  
- **Aim = reliably and efficiently**  
- **Observation = reveals state and action**  
- **Action = should match state**  
- **Failure = misfit between state and action**
