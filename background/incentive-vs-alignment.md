## Incentive Conflict vs Alignment

### Why this matters now

Teams often assume execution gaps come from poor alignment, so they increase communication. But when behaviour is driven by incentives or constraints, this leads to repeated “agreement without change.”
Getting this wrong creates wasted effort and persistent divergence — getting it right shifts focus to structural fixes.

### Core claim

Execution divergence comes from two different conditions that look similar but behave differently. In alignment issues, people act differently because they understand the situation differently. In incentive conflict, people understand the goal but are constrained or rewarded to act otherwise. This distinction matters because alignment fixes rely on shared understanding, while incentive conflicts require changes to metrics, ownership, or risk structures.

### 5 key questions this doc answers

* When is divergence caused by misunderstanding vs constraint?
* What observable signals indicate incentive conflict rather than misalignment?
* How can you test which condition you’re in?
* Why does alignment effort fail in incentive-driven systems?
* What types of intervention actually change behaviour?

### What to look for (observable signals)

* Signal: Agreement in meetings, but consistent deviation in execution
* Signal: Behaviour aligns with local metrics rather than stated goals
* Signal: Repeated clarification does not change outcomes
* Signal: Different teams optimise for different success measures
* Signal: People justify deviations in terms of risk, targets, or accountability

### What this means for action

* If signals are strong: shift focus from alignment to incentives, governance, and constraints
* If signals are mixed: test behaviour under clarified goals to see what changes
* If confidence is low: probe with small changes to metrics or incentives
* If value is unclear: question whether the goal itself is shared or worth pursuing

### Common misread

People interpret divergence as lack of clarity because disagreement is visible, while incentives are often implicit. This leads to more communication, workshops, and alignment sessions. The result is “alignment theatre” — apparent agreement without behavioural change — which increases frustration and slows progress while leaving the real constraint untouched.

### Fast test

* Restate the goal clearly and observe behaviour change
* Temporarily adjust a metric or incentive and observe behaviour change
* Remove or reduce a constraint (e.g. approval, risk exposure) and observe behaviour change

### Example in practice

* Situation: Central team defines a standard process across regions
* Signals: Regions agree in meetings but continue using local variations
* Interpretation: Behaviour is driven by local performance metrics and risk ownership
* Action: Adjust regional metrics and clarify accountability boundaries
* Result: Behaviour begins to converge without additional alignment effort

### Boundaries

* This concept is not: general disagreement or poor communication
* Do not apply when: teams genuinely describe different problems or goals
* Adjacent concept to check: misalignment (lack of shared understanding)
* Adjacent concept to check: local optimisation vs system optimisation
