# Investment vs Fit — Background

## Why this exists

Teams often increase effort when results are unclear.

More people, more budget, more activity.

But effort is not the constraint.

The real question is whether the **type of action fits the system state**, and whether the work is **worth doing at all**.

Without this distinction, systems drift into:

* high activity with low impact
* continued investment in low-value work
* scaling misfit rather than improving outcomes

---

## The core interaction

Two things must be considered together:

* **Fit**
  (does the type of action match the system state?)

* **Investment**
  (how much effort, time, and resource is being applied?)

On their own, each can look reasonable.

Together, they determine whether effort creates value — or waste.

---

## What actually happens

When investment increases without fit:

* activity rises, but outcomes do not
* teams optimise the wrong things
* problems persist or shift rather than resolve
* effort becomes harder to justify, but continues

This doesn’t look like “bad decisions”.

It looks like:

* “we need to push harder”
* “we’ve already invested a lot”
* “we can’t stop now”

---

## Why it gets missed

Investment decisions are often based on:

* sunk cost (“we’ve come this far”)
* urgency (“we need to show progress”)
* visibility (“this is a priority programme”)

Rather than:

* whether the work is valuable (Stop context)
* whether the action fits the state (stabilise, align, probe, optimise)

So effort increases even when:

* value is unclear
* the system is not ready
* the action type is wrong

---

## Practical framing

Before increasing investment, separate two questions:

> **Should we be doing this at all?** (value)

> **Is this the right kind of action for the current state?** (fit)

Only then ask:

> **How much effort does this deserve?**

---

## Common misfit patterns

* **High investment in Stop contexts**
  → improving or scaling work that is not clearly valuable

* **High investment in misalignment (Align)**
  → driving delivery or standardisation without shared understanding

* **High investment in uncertainty (Probe)**
  → running large programmes instead of learning

* **High investment in instability (Stabilise)**
  → optimising or automating before control exists

In each case:

> effort increases, but system outcomes do not improve

---

## Failure pattern: activity replaces progress

A common signal is when:

* work expands
* reporting increases
* visible effort rises

But:

* system behaviour does not change
* outcomes remain inconsistent
* underlying issues persist

Progress becomes **inferred from activity**, not observed in results.

---

## Sunk-cost continuation

Investment can continue for reasons unrelated to value.

Signals include:

* justification based on past investment, not current outcomes
* reluctance to stop despite weak evidence of impact
* shifting success criteria to maintain momentum
* continued effort where usage or value is unclear

This indicates:

> effort is being protected, not value created

---

## Evidence for increasing investment

Investment should increase only when there is evidence that:

* the work creates outcomes that are actually used
* results are becoming more consistent or reliable
* the type of action matches the system state
* additional effort is likely to produce more value (not more activity)

Without this:

> increasing investment amplifies waste

---

## The rule (simple form)

* **Do not increase investment where value is unclear (Stop)**
* **Do not increase investment where action does not fit the state**
* **Increase investment only when both value and fit are evident**

Or more practically:

> Investment should follow **evidence of value and correct fit**, not precede it.

---

## What this protects against

This logic protects against:

* scaling low-value work
* reinforcing misalignment or instability
* locking in incorrect approaches
* wasting effort on problems not yet understood

---

## Link to DRIFT thinking

This sits directly on two core checks:

* **Should we act?** (value and context clarity)
* **What kind of action fits?** (capability state)

Investment is applied **after** these are clear.

If they are wrong:

* more investment → more waste

If they are right:

* more investment → more value

---

## A useful test

Before increasing effort:

> “Is this improving the system — or just increasing activity?”

The answer should be visible in system behaviour, not plans or intent.

---

## One tension to hold

Reducing investment can feel like slowing down or losing momentum.

But when value or fit is unclear:

* **more effort → more waste**

So the real trade-off is:

* **visible effort vs actual impact**
